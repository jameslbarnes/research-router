## 7:15 a.m., Pittsburgh

Her group at Carnegie Mellon produces results that move the whole field. And the instrument she reaches for first is a $20-a-month consumer plan: a fine instrument for drafting an essay, the wrong one for the frontier of cryptography. Widen the lens once and her Tuesday repeats across science. ## What her Tuesday leaves behind

By 6 p.m. Elaine has done a full day of some of the most valuable thinking on earth. She weighed three candidate constructions for a differentially oblivious join and ruled out two of them: one because the padding it needs grows quadratically in the data size, one because its noise mechanism leaks structure under composition. She spent forty minutes at the whiteboard arguing a definitional choice with her PhD student, which neighboring relation the privacy guarantee should bind to, and the student's counterexample changed her mind. That argument may be the sharpest piece of cryptographic reasoning produced in Pittsburgh today.

Now count what survives it. The paper, eight months out, will record the winning construction and its proof, and the record will hold that much faithfully. The two dead constructions compress to half a sentence in a related-work paragraph. The whiteboard argument survives as one revised definition, with the counterexample that forced it living on in exactly two memories. A paper is the fossil of an idea: the shape that held, pressed flat, with the living argument evaporated out of it. Cyan Banister's phrase for the broader pattern is that we are building the future in disappearing ink, and science is the purest case. We archive the outputs with real care, in journals, proceedings, and repositories, while the reasoning behind them fades by default, every day, at every whiteboard, in every chat session that scrolls away.

Widen the lens once more and the surviving record itself is under strain. Retractions crossed ten thousand in a single recent year, synthetic text is entering the literature faster than referees can read it, and reviewer load keeps climbing while the incentive structure rewards volume. So preservation alone is half a fix: whatever we keep has to carry its own proof of integrity, because the corpus it joins is losing the benefit of the doubt. Civilization has solved this problem before, on purpose. When the record mattered enough, we built scriptoria: institutions whose entire job was to copy the text, check it letter by letter, and sign for its fidelity. The Research Router is a scriptorium for the reasoning of science, and its whole charter fits in three verbs.

**Preserve.** Keep the reasoning a researcher approves, in her own words, beside the result: the two constructions she killed and why, the counterexample that moved the definition.

**Verify.** Attach provenance to every entry, ground its citations so they hold when pulled, and attest the pipeline in hardware, so trust in the record becomes something a reader checks rather than assumes.

**Route.** Preserved and verified, the reasoning becomes a live map of each field, and the same substrate that holds Elaine's thinking can carry it to the one person and the one question where it matters most, so work finds work.

The loss cuts deepest exactly where the tools are thinnest: the people producing the record most worth keeping are running it through the interface least able to keep it. The answer meets both gaps in one move. Put the agentic frontier in researchers' hands, and make preservation the same motion as using it. That is the offer, and the next section prices it.

## Her day, rebuilt hour by hour

The seat has to win Tuesday on merit. Elaine already pays $20 a month for a general-purpose window; the seat upgrades her to Claude Max, and the Router has to make that upgrade win the specific hours of her actual day. So here is the day, job by job.

**07:30, the digest.** Coffee, laptop, and a briefing that read overnight arXiv postings against her open drafts, her coauthors' recent work, and the threads she has marked live. Each entry says why this paper matters to that draft: this one tightens a bound she cites, this one attacks an assumption in her section 4. Ten minutes replaces the forty she used to spend triaging feeds, and the ten minutes are grounded in her work rather than the field's noise.

**Through the morning, her corpus on tap.** Her papers, her coauthors' papers, and the record around them, indexed and queryable, with citations that resolve to the actual passage rather than a hallucinated venue. When a student asks whether the 2019 construction handled adaptive corruptions, the answer arrives with the theorem number.

**After lunch, as she writes.** Related work surfaces at the moment of use: she drafts a paragraph on noise mechanisms and the agent offers the paper she should cite there, with the sentence of hers it supports. The literature review becomes a running companion instead of a two-day slog at the end.

**Before submission, the committee in rehearsal.** She sends the draft through reviewer simulation and gets back each likely reviewer's angle: the skeptic on concrete efficiency, the theorist who will ask why the relaxation is the right one. She hardens the draft against the committee before the committee exists.

**When a sub-problem sits outside her wheelhouse.** Her differential-obliviousness draft raises a concrete-parameters question about discrete-Gaussian sampling, a lattice-algorithmics matter rather than a cryptography one. She hands that piece to a matched skill, or to a matched remote collaborator (section 6 shows the engine that finds one), and keeps her own attention on the core argument.

**And the sixth job, the one senior researchers name first when asked what they want from an agent: the filter.** Her door already overflows. Talks, reviews, committees, cold emails, each individually reasonable, collectively fatal to deep work. The mechanism is concrete: an MCP-connected triage agent over her inbox and calendar that scores each inbound request against priorities she states once, drafts the declines for her approval, and books the rare interruption genuinely worth taking. It ships in the pilot's second quarter, and for a researcher at her level it is the job that makes the other five sustainable.

One design constraint governs all six, and it came from an early design-partner researcher: protect the core whiteboard loop, the irreducibly human 15 percent where the actual thinking happens, and transform the ancillary 85 percent, the literature triage, the formatting, the review prep, the correspondence, the coordination. The Router earns its seat in the 85 and keeps its hands off the 15.

And here is the design's central move, stated once, plainly: each of these jobs ends by depositing an approved, first-person summary of the reasoning behind it. Why this citation and that framing, which reviewer objection reshaped the proof, why the parameters question got handed off. She reads it, edits it, approves it in her own name, and it flows to her sovereign store. The tool that wins her mornings is the same instrument that fills the commons. Adoption and preservation are one motion: she gets her day back, and her field gets the reasoning that every prior generation of tools let evaporate.

## What each day deposits, and who holds the keys

Every job in Elaine's rebuilt Tuesday leaves a residue: the reasoning behind the work, wrong turns included. That residue is the deposit the whole memo runs on, and Elaine is a cryptographer, so her first question about it is custody, asked with professional teeth. Yours should be too. Here is the loop, one step per line, with the enforcement named at each step:

1. **Capture in full.** Complete session logs, dead ends and all, land in her own TinyCloud encrypted Space: client-side AES-256-GCM, UCAN-scoped delegations she can revoke, keys held by her alone. Raw logs stay hers; what leaves her machine is what she approves.
2. **Provenance, public now.** A timestamped, attested commitment publishes immediately, proving the reasoning existed on that date and stays unaltered since. This is a different instrument from a preprint: arXiv timestamps outputs, and it proves priority only by publishing the content. The sealed commitment proves priority and integrity on reasoning she is months from ready to show, which is the direct answer to the fifty-year priority economy the opening described.
3. **Route privately.** The matching engine reads the sealed record inside an attested Intel TDX enclave, so she gets full-context routing while the raw work stays encrypted end to end. Researchers who want a harder floor run degraded mode: routing reads only their approved summaries, and raw logs never leave the local machine.
4. **Open on her timeline.** The content enters the public commons when she chooses: on publication, on a time-lock, or by opt-in, with approve-to-post in her own name through router-daybook. To be precise about what is mandatory: contributing approved reasoning to the commons is the price of the seat; licensing anything beyond the commons, including the trace corpus described in section 8, is separately opt-in, revocable, off by default, and declining it never affects renewal.
5. **Renew by contributing.** The rule is stated, so it can be audited: the seat renews when the researcher approves reasoning deposits tied to at least four distinct captured sessions in the month, each passing the substance-and-sensitivity check already running in the commons, with the threshold and the check both published. The enclave attests that the pipeline ran on real sessions and emits a signed contribution report each cycle; renewal is executed against that report, so the funder audits evidence rather than slides. Substance beyond the check is judged the only two ways it can be: by the researcher signing her name, and by the downstream ratings her contributions earn.

Each piece of this runs today rather than on a roadmap. The teleport-router commons is live at router.teleport.computer: an attested TEE with staged publishing and a protocol-level sensitivity check on everything that moves toward the feed. The router-daybook desktop app does local-first capture with approval before anything leaves her machine; router-teamwork carries the team layer; TinyCloud carries custody. A funder doing diligence can request the attestation quote and verify the exact code handling the data before a seat is funded.

State the threat model plainly, because Elaine's community writes the side-channel papers. Attestation is evidence, and evidence has scope: content is encrypted client-side and decrypts only inside the enclave during routing, so an enclave compromise exposes the sessions routed during the compromise window and leaves the sealed stores intact; degraded mode shrinks even that surface to approved summaries. The pilot invites exactly this audit, and treats it as iterative: findings from the cohort's own security review are fixed, re-attested, and published, and the loop is judged on how it hardens under scrutiny from the people best qualified to break it. If the exchange holds for the most security-conscious researchers alive, it holds for everyone downstream of them.

This is also, word for word, the tooling ARIA's Scaling Trust programme describes: agents that "securely coordinate, negotiate, and verify with one another on our behalf" (ARIA, Trust: everything, everywhere). Competitive, high-stakes academia, where the parties guard real value from one another and cooperate anyway when the guarantees hold, is the hardest honest testbed that charter could ask for. ## The Tuesday the commons pays her back

Everything in the last two sections flows one way: Elaine deposits reasoning into the commons. This section is about the current flowing back, and it rests on one of the most robust findings in the science-of-science literature. A study of 9.2 million papers found that work drawing on subdisciplines far apart on the map of science earns the highest relative citation counts, and that 69.9% of cross-disciplinary pairings raised impact for both fields, against 3.3% for neither.[^route-idr] Routing between distant researchers is among the highest-leverage interventions available on the research system, and it is exactly the intervention nobody's calendar has room for. So we built the router and ran it, end to end, on live data, with Elaine's public graph as the anchor.

Full disclosure before the results, because a diligent reader checks provenance first. The run used only public data: Elaine's OpenAlex coauthorship graph, published papers, and public roles. She is the anchor of the analysis because her community sets the security bar this system was built to meet; she has not deposited a session log or endorsed this memo, and recruiting her, or a researcher of her standing in the same community, is the pilot's first task. The vetting panel described below is a panel of LLM reviewer agents with web search: a cheap, reproducible critic rather than human judgment, which is why milestone M2 makes human ratings the bar. And before the funding call happens, we run the one experiment that outranks every percentage in this section: the top-ranked introduction goes out to both parties, and I bring their answers to the call.

Here is what the run produced. A paper in her morning digest leads to a person. The engine walked her real coauthorship graph two hops out: all 314 of her direct coauthors walked and set aside (she knows them already, and six split OpenAlex profiles of those same coauthors were caught and dropped too), 49,613 coauthors-of-coauthors fanned out, 300 best-bridged candidates kept, 137 cleared a shared-thread gate, 7 survived the reviewer-agent panel. Each finalist was scored on productive distance (the `overlap × (1 − overlap)` curve, where near-duplicates and total strangers both lose to genuine bridges) and cross-field reach, then discounted by an already-met prior built from academic genealogy, community co-membership, and bridge distance. That discount matters because the goal is discovery: an introduction to someone the anchor already knows adds nothing. Citations turn out to be a poor proxy for acquaintance, so the prior reads the signals citations miss. One of those signals is IC3 co-membership; Elaine is IC3's Chief Scientist, a detail some readers of this memo will appreciate firsthand.

The discount inverts the naive ranking, and the inversion is the whole point. Paweł Szałachowski cites Elaine in at least 29 distinct papers and looks like an obvious match; the vetting found he spent four years in her PhD advisor's group and now works with her longtime IC3 collaborator, so an introduction would route entirely inside her own network, and he drops off the list. David Woodruff, the strongest topical bridge left standing, is her own CMU department colleague; she can walk down the hall. The top find is Junbin Fang, a professor of optoelectronic engineering at Jinan University in Guangzhou who designs quantum Byzantine-agreement protocols and runs a working photonics lab, QKD over 150 km of fiber. The panel puts the chance they have already met at 7%, the lowest on the list, and the shared problem is the one she bounded classically: his entanglement-based protocols escape the classical one-third fault bound in the detectable relaxation ("This type of protocol is unsolvable in the classical field, but it can be solved using quantum physics properties"), while her lower bounds ("Rethinking Large-Scale Consensus") say exactly what escaping costs. His group cites her in two papers; she cites him in none that OpenAlex's indexed references can see, and since reference coverage is partial, that zero is a floor, not a fact. Two communities, one problem, and until this run, no bridge.

The engine then does the part a conference hallway never does: it hands both parties a discussion draft with a scoped joint problem, every claim anchored in direct quotes from papers on both sides. For Shi and Fang the realistic first paper is what quantum resources actually buy in Byzantine agreement: a model paper that places his entanglement-based protocols inside her adversary taxonomy (does an entangled channel behave like a PKI, like trusted hardware, or like something genuinely new?), gives his lab's protocols their first rigorous security analysis, and takes a small photonic validation from his lab as the experimental section. The draft is honest about the risk: his consensus papers live in optics journals without the adversary models her community demands, and the pairing works only if he wants his protocols audited rather than celebrated, which is precisely why it needs a system to hand both people the specific problem.

One consistency check, not a validation, since both columns are heuristics on a seven-row table: the cheap graph-only prior and the reviewer-agent panel pick the same standout stranger (the prior reads Fang at 25% already-met, its lowest genealogy-and-community signal on the roster; the panel, after web-checking the pairing, says 7%). Where they disagree, the misses run through exactly what a coauthorship graph cannot see: the panel reads Deepak Garg at 60% because he and Shi were PhD students in the same CMU department, while the graph prior says 23%; venue and program-committee co-occurrence is the next signal queued. The judgment that counts, though, is human, and it is built into the product: every match ships with a rating loop. Elaine tells the router whether the Fang introduction was worth her hour, and that rating trains the already-met prior and the distance scoring for everyone downstream. Each contributor's traces sharpen the map for the whole commons, so the value of the network rises with N, which is the same N that prices the pilot. The seat buys her the daily tools; the commons pays her back in the collaborator she would never have found alone.

## From introduction to publication: the flywheel

An introduction needs a room. So the Router convenes one: an agent-matched conference, colocated with an IEEE or ACM venue the field already respects (S&P, CCS, SODA), with modest travel stipends so attending costs a researcher a day rather than a grant line. Colocation buys immediate standing; the pairings are ours. The logistics respect the calendar: affiliated-event proposals run six to nine months ahead of these venues, so the proposal goes in during the pilot's first quarter for a panel in months ten to twelve.

The ritual runs on a two-year arc. Year one, the matched pair shares a panel, and the chair hands them the scoped problem the synthesis already drafted: for a Shi-Fang panel, what quantum resources buy in Byzantine agreement, with a rigorous security analysis of his lab's existing protocols as the low-glamour, high-certainty deliverable. Year two, when the panel clicks, they present the paper at the next edition. The gap between panel and paper is the point: a public, dated commitment engages a researcher's pride where a stipend never reaches, and it gives the funder a falsifiable checkpoint (did the pairing produce a submission, yes or no). Engineering serendipity this way has a lineage, from the Macy Conferences through the Santa Fe Institute, both of which produced whole fields by putting distant disciplines in one room on purpose.

The arc also turns the matching engine into a supply chain. The anchor run walked 49,613 two-hop candidates down to 7 vetted finalists; every pilot researcher gets the same funnel, so a 25-seat cohort yields a full panel slate in year one without a program committee. And each edition restocks the pipeline, because the loop closes on itself: daily use feeds the commons, the commons feeds the matching, the matching convenes the conference, the conference produces papers, and the papers pull the next cohort in, each new researcher arriving with a coauthor's endorsement rather than our pitch. That compounding is what a $200 seat is actually priced against, which brings us to the money.
