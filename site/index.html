The Research Router

Researchers do better work when their AI agents are grounded in their community.

Most researchers use AI through consumer chat tools on cheap tiers while frontier research environments stay locked inside a few funded labs — and the researchers who do adopt AI grow more narrow and more isolated, not less. We think two changes together reverse that: put frontier tools in researchers' hands, and ground them in each researcher's community, so every session points outward — toward a colleague's result or a collaborator two fields away. It is a falsifiable claim, tested with a randomized, blind-scored trial — and the project continues only if grounding beats the ungrounded frontier.

The hypothesis ↓ The experiment
Dedicated to the common understanding of all humanity
The argument, in five sentences
  1. Researchers who use AI publish more and get cited more, but they talk to fewer colleagues and explore fewer topics. That is the finding across 41.3 million papers.
  2. We think that happens because today's AI tools know the published literature but nothing about the researcher's community — who they work with, and who is working on the same problem right now.
  3. Our hypothesis: connect the AI to that community — the researcher's own papers, their collaborators, and the people just beyond them — and the work gets measurably better.
  4. We test it with a randomized trial: 25 researchers and 25 grad students, comparing a basic chat subscription, a funded frontier seat, and that same seat plus the Router. If the Router doesn't win, the project stops.
  5. If it does win, the result is the case for the real goal: funders underwriting unlimited frontier AI for PhD researchers and grad students — a network strong enough that the best minds don't have to join a frontier lab to work at the frontier.
Why research falls behind

Research is getting more isolated,
right when it should be getting more connected

Across 41.3 million papers, researchers who adopt AI publish 3.02× the papers and earn 4.84× the citations, but they engage with 22% less of their fellow scientists' work and cover 4.63% fewer topics. The authors call it "lonely crowds": ungrounded AI makes each person louder and the room emptier.
This is the whole problem in one line. The tools that should be pulling researchers together are pulling them apart, right when the science needs them talking to each other. The three forces behind it come next; the rest of the page is what to do about it.

Most researchers reach for the plain chat window. ChatGPT alone accounted for 82% of AI-using developers in the 2025 Stack Overflow survey, roughly double any coding assistant or agent, and among scientists the pattern holds: 77.5% name a general-purpose chat tool as their primary tool. Hardly any of them pay for it. One campus survey put 88% of AI users on the free tier, and that is starting to matter, because the paid frontier is pulling away fast and access to it is becoming an advantage money can buy. Tools like Anthropic's Claude Science are a real step up from the chat window, and the $200-a-month seat that unlocks them sits above what most grants are written to buy.

Even the best tool leaves this in place, because any answer drawn from a model's weights carries two kinds of error at once. The precision failures are the famous ones: invented citations, confident claims no source supports. The recall failures are quieter and cost more: the weights end at the training data, so the model cannot surface the coauthor who posted something relevant last week, the preprint that breaks one of your assumptions, the distant lab circling the same question — the newest, closest work is exactly what is missing. And every private answer, right or wrong, replaces a question the researcher would have taken to a colleague. Consumer or frontier, the tool is grounded in the literature and blind to the room.

Incentives make it worse. Careers ride on getting there first, so sharing a half-formed idea is a way to get scooped, and the real conversation stays among a handful of trusted people until the paper is out. And the paper keeps only the answer. The wrong turns, the arguments that actually changed someone's mind, live on in two or three memories and nowhere else. The investor Cyan Banister calls it building "the most consequential technology in human history… in disappearing ink": Darwin left notebooks and Einstein left decades of correspondence, but their successors work it out in DMs and group chats that will not survive the decade. If anything the picture is one step worse than her essay draws it, because the deepest reasoning is moving out of group chats entirely and into LLM threads — one researcher and one model in a private channel — ink that disappears before it ever had a second reader. Preservation, she argues, has never happened without deliberate institutional effort. That is what the Router is.

There is a longer arc here too. Science began at letter-writing speed, traded that speed for vetting, and has been clawing it back ever since; the Router is the next step on the curve — active work at t = 0, with priority attested and the record kept.

The evidence on this page is collected in Large Language Models in Academic Research, a literature review covering roughly seventy empirical, bibliometric, and position sources on how researchers actually use AI.
Its two findings that matter here: frontier models are becoming central to individual research work, and each researcher uses one alone, in a private channel — which at population scale shows up as fewer collaborators and narrower topics. Read the full review (PDF).

Headline figures, each checked against its primary source: Arroyo-Machado et al., PLOS ONE 2025 (65% of US academic STEM scientists have used generative AI in teaching or research) · O'Brien et al., arXiv:2512.19644 (77.5% of AI-adopting scientists who program name a general-purpose chat tool as primary) · Stanford HAI (51 notable ML models from industry vs. academia's 15 in 2023) · Khandelwal et al., arXiv:2410.23261 (academic labs typically work with 1 to 8 GPUs) · Gofman & Jin, Journal of Finance 2024 (211 tenure-line AI professors left North American universities 2004-2018; the leavers' citation share rose from about 5% to nearly 19%) · Edwards & Roy 2017 (perverse incentives and hypercompetition) · Hao et al., Nature 2025 (across 41.3M papers, AI-adopting researchers cut scientist-to-scientist engagement 22% and narrowed collective topics 4.63%) · Oxford University Press 2024 (76% of 2,345 researchers already use an AI tool, most commonly translation and chatbots) · Wiley ExplanAItions 2025 (researcher AI use rose from 57% to 84% in a year) · Stack Overflow 2025 (ChatGPT used by 82% of AI-using developers, ~2x any coding-specific tool) · Contractor & Reyes 2025 (of AI users at an elite college, 88% were on free tiers; only 11% paid) · Walters & Wilder, Scientific Reports 2023 (of 636 citations in ChatGPT-generated literature reviews, 55% of GPT-3.5's and 18% of GPT-4's were fabricated).

What we think fixes it

Grounding an agent in its researcher's community measurably improves the work

Adding a community-grounding layer to a frontier research environment produces (1) higher-quality work, (2) higher researcher satisfaction, and (3) more serendipitous connections than the same environment without it.
The grounding lives entirely in context and tool design: what the agent can reach and what it can do. No model is fine-tuned. That is what makes it testable with a ladder of arms that each differ from the next by exactly one thing: the consumer plan, the funded frontier seat, and that seat plus the Router.

The default picture of AI in research — ask a model to one-shot a theorem or a paper — is the smallest corner of the design space. At frontier capability the researcher's job is pointing the model into territory it would not reach alone, and one researcher covers one basin of attention. A network of researchers with overlapping but distinct interests, each steering their own grounded agent, covers the field: a group chat instead of a private channel. That is the mechanism the Router bets on; the trial measures whether the bet pays.

"Grounded in their community" means the agent has the researcher's own papers, their coauthors' papers, and the network one hop beyond them as working context, plus tools that act on that context: surfacing relevant work, protecting priority so early sharing is safe, and turning the live work just outside the researcher's own circle into threads worth pulling on. The trial section's three-arm ladder separates what frontier access is worth from what grounding adds — the two numbers this page turns on.

This also answers the obvious objection. If ungrounded AI is what thins the room, why would more AI help? Because the isolation comes from a tool that keeps every question and answer between the researcher and the model. Grounding plus approve-to-post keeps the researcher in the loop, editing and signing what the agent keeps. That human-in-the-loop mode is the one the evidence shows both raises quality and preserves the researcher's own expertise, where handing the work off wholesale erodes both. The lonely-crowd result is a design choice. Build the tool to point outward and the outcome flips. Grounding is the single variable this trial isolates.

What Claude Science does

Claude Science is a real leap,
and it works on one scientist at a time

Anthropic's Claude Science is the strongest research environment a frontier lab has shipped. It pulls a scientist's scattered workflow into one place: it reads the literature and runs multi-step analysis, renders 3D protein structures and genome tracks, hands off the HPC and GPU jobs, wires in more than sixty curated tools for fields from genomics to cheminformatics, and runs a reviewer agent that checks every citation and calculation. Each output carries an auditable trail you can reproduce. For the individual researcher, it is a genuine step up.

The leap

One scientist, fully equipped

Data to figure in a single session, sixty-plus domain tools, the heavy compute handled, every citation and calculation checked. The individual researcher's workflow, done well and done in one place.

The gap

The room between scientists

Spend a week inside it and three limits surface. It is packaged: a shelf of pre-built skills, and a working researcher we asked to evaluate it put it plainly — I see how I would do this myself, so I don't want it abstracted away. It is passive: even its literature search waits for you to arrive with a fully formed question, and the questions that change a research program are the ones you don't yet know to ask. And it is single-player: nothing in it knows your coauthors, your live threads, or the person two fields over circling your problem. The result is an adoption paradox — the researchers strong enough to use it tend not to need it, and the ones who would gain most have often never heard of it.

None of this is an argument against the workbench. It is the argument for the Router: take Claude Science as the foundation, subsidize the seat, and add the one thing it cannot see — the room. Claude Science makes the individual scientist rigorous; the Router makes the field around them connected, and it does so inside Claude Science and the other tools a researcher already opens. Here is what it adds.

What the Router adds

Claude Science plus the Router keeps your research
fresh, grounded, and collaborative

The Router is a loop that runs once a day: the morning digest points you at what moved, your sessions run grounded in your field, what they settle flows into the commons, and the commons feeds everyone's next digest. Everything rides over MCP (the open protocol that lets tools plug into AI agents) inside the tools you already open. The six cards after the loop are what each turn of it adds to a working day.

01

Insights every morning about the people and ideas around you

Overnight, the agent reads new arXiv postings, the latest work of your coauthors and the people one hop beyond them, and fresh entries in the commons — not just results, but the library a colleague's session vetted or the link two grad students both found useful — all against your own open drafts and live threads. By the time you sit down you have a short digest: this result tightens a bound you lean on, that preprint pushes on an assumption in your section 4, this researcher two fields over is circling the same question you are. The promise, from day one: if something meaningful is moving in your field while you are working on it, it reaches you. Researchers who turned off ePrint (cryptography's arXiv) because the volume beat them get the room back, filtered, with every line tied to their own work.

tests: quality · satisfaction
02

Your whole field as working memory

Your papers, your coauthors' papers, the network one hop beyond them, and the record underneath it all, indexed and queryable, with every citation resolving to the actual passage — surfaced as you draft, so the reference that belongs in a paragraph arrives while you are writing it. That is the gap between a confident guess and a checkable answer: Walters & Wilder analyzed 636 citations in ChatGPT-generated literature reviews and found 55% of GPT-3.5's and 18% of GPT-4's fabricated outright, often under real journal names. Trust is the binding constraint at the frontier, and a corpus where every claim lands on a real passage is how you earn it.

tests: quality · grounding accuracy
03

Face the committee before it exists

Before you submit, the draft runs through a simulated panel that plays each likely reviewer: the skeptic on concrete efficiency, the theorist who asks why this relaxation is the right one. You meet the hard objections early, while there is still room to answer them inside the paper.

tests: quality
04

Keep the reasoning while it is still fresh

The dead ends and the killed constructions — why one approach failed, which counterexample moved a definition — usually evaporate by the next morning. Captured as entries you edit and sign, they become a durable record you own, in your own words and under your own name.

tests: satisfaction
05

Share early and keep the credit

Cryptography already plays this game explicitly, on ePrint. You post when the work can hold your name: early enough to claim credit, never so early that a retracted theorem tarnishes it — and the moment you hear a rival is on your problem, you rush something up just to timestamp yourself. The Router does not create that game; it changes the payoff. Each approved entry carries a timestamped cryptographic commitment — the entry's hash anchored publicly, provable later without revealing the entry, while the TEE attests the custody pipeline — so the timestamp holds your priority without demanding a finished draft. A peer-reviewed proceeding typically reaches the field three or four months after the work is done, an ePrint posting about a month; a committed entry exists at t = 0. Cryptographers already act as if ideas arrive rather than originate — simultaneous discovery is the norm — and the timestamp is how you plant the flag without publishing the map. The people with the most to gain are the ones with the most at risk today: grad students with no reputation yet to trade on.

tests: satisfaction · serendipity
06

The conversations hiding just outside your view

The Router does not propose meetings; nobody wants another coffee with a stranger, however well-matched. It reads the live work of your network — your coauthors and the people one hop beyond them — against your own, and surfaces the collisions as substance: you might talk about X and Y, with the specific results attached. Not "here is a person worth meeting" but "this bound of yours and that protocol of theirs are the same conversation." Because the commons holds work as it is produced, the thread lands mid-problem — not a year later in each other's published abstracts — and whether it becomes an email, a shared draft, or a joint paper is yours to decide; either way it starts from shared substance, not small talk.

tests: serendipity

Every one of these gets sharper as models improve: today the digest surfaces the relevant paper; a stronger model will reason about the exact argument it unsettles in your draft. The grounding lives in context and tools, not in the model, so the same layer rides each new release the day it lands. It is also why the trial measures a delta — the grounded seat against the identical seat without the Router — so the result holds through model turnover.

The confidentiality commitment
Every feature above asks you to hand an agent your live, unpublished reasoning, and no cryptographer should take that on faith. So confidential compute is a design commitment, not a roadmap item: running your working thoughts through a leaky tool is scooping yourself, and if private inference were not viable, this proposal would not exist.
The first rung is built, and you can ask for the attestation quote: your keys stay with you, entries route through an attested TEE, a sensitivity check runs at capture before you are ever asked to sign, and approved entries keep a grace window in which you can still delete them. Nothing reaches another researcher except through the approve-to-post gate, and the privacy budget points outward — rate-limit information out, not queries in — so the agent can read your work constantly and never speak of it. The rungs above (practical private inference, obfuscation and ORAM-based retrieval at tolerable cost) are open research problems, which is one more reason to anchor in cryptography: this trust layer is an agenda the anchor group's own students can take up.
Two open questions
The access question

Who funds the frontier seat?

Today's easy access to the frontier could close. The most capable models — Claude's Fable class today — sit at the top of a widening price ladder, and the free tier at the bottom looks like a land-grab. As it ends, the best tools may settle behind prices that outrun academic budgets, and the frontier stays in reach only when someone funds the seat.

The precedents are accumulating: in February 2026 Anthropic gave up to 10,000 open-source maintainers six months of its $200-a-month Claude Max tier for free, and it now offers verified K-12 teachers a full year of premium access at no cost — deliberate subsidies of communities that sit outside commercial pricing. Academic researchers are the same kind of community, still without their equivalent, and this pilot is a first, measured version of one.

The attribution question

Who gets credit for a relayed idea?

The commons runs on contributed reasoning, and a system that takes your thinking without returning credit deserves to fail. When the Router relays your killed construction into someone else's proof, the coauthor list has no row for it. So the pilot ships the primitive any answer needs: each time a surfaced item is confirmed useful, one signed edge — who informed what — lands on the same attested trail that carries the commons. The trial collects these edges from day one.

Two things get built on that log: a Router score — how often your work surfaced in others' sessions and what it informed, connective work the citation record has never counted — and a new kind of acknowledgement section, parallel to the coauthor list, that fits at the end of a paper.

How we test it · three arms, one community, blind-scored

The consumer plan, the frontier,
and the frontier grounded

Two cohorts in parallel — 25 established researchers and 25 grad students — in one field's community, each randomized across three arms that form a ladder. Each arm differs from the next by one thing, so the trial reads out two numbers: what putting a scientist at the frontier is worth, and what grounding that frontier in their community adds on top. In retrieval terms: the step from A to B mostly buys precision — hallucination is a largely solved problem, just unevenly distributed. The step from B to C is after recall: the results, people, and threads moving in your field that would never have reached you.

Arm A · baseline

A consumer chat plan

The general-purpose chat interface most AI-using scientists rely on today, provisioned at the standard consumer tier (around $20/mo). This arm anchors the trial to the field's real starting point.

Arm B · the frontier

A funded frontier seat

A funded $200/mo Claude Max seat carrying Claude Code and Anthropic's Claude Science workbench. Frontier access, no grounding. The gap from Arm A is what the seat subsidy alone buys.

Arm C · grounded

The seat, plus the Router

Everything in Arm B, plus the grounded corpus and the feature set above, delivered over MCP into the same tools. The gap from Arm B is the headline: what community grounding adds.

Two cohorts in parallel
The trial runs two cohorts in parallel: 25 established researchers and 25 grad students, matched and randomized across the same three arms. Grounding gets measured twice — once for the researcher with a network, once for the researcher still building one — and if it does anything, it should do the most for the one with capability and no reputation yet.
The grad-student cohort is not an add-on; it is where the stakes are highest. The real promise of a PhD is apprenticeship into a community, and that apprenticeship runs through the advisor's network: a student learns to socialize in year three or four, when the conference introductions finally start. Now follow the numbers above. Researchers who adopt AI engage with 22% less of their fellow scientists' work and need fewer hands for the same work, and no one forces a PI to take on grad students. Extrapolate, and it is hard to see what a PhD program can promise its incoming cohort unless something about how young researchers get connected changes. That is the change this trial prices: the Router puts the connective machinery in year one, exposure scaled to the work, feedback either way it lands. For the trial window the doubled cohort's marginal cost is small — twenty-five more seats, most at $200 and a third at the consumer tier — next to the statistical power it buys.
Primary · blinded

Quality of real work

Sampled outputs from each researcher's actual sessions, scored by raters kept blind to which arm produced them. These are real drafts, reviews, and analyses from the cohort's own work. We score executed work: AI ideas can rate more novel than experts' and then lose that edge once someone builds them out, so execution is what counts.

Secondary · mechanical

Grounding accuracy

Fraction of citations and claims that resolve to a real source saying what the output claims; fraction of person-references that are real, correctly attributed, and new to the researcher.

Secondary · reported

Satisfaction and serendipity

Self-reported usefulness, plus a count of the connections that surfaced on their own: citations, people, and threads the researcher confirms were new and useful. It is listed as self-reported because self-report misleads here. Across studies, researchers report productivity gains that blind measurement fails to confirm, so we treat perceived usefulness as a signal and let the blind score decide.

Measuring recall · the survey
Recall gets measured the way ad-lift studies measure memory: show people things and ask what stuck. At the end of the window, every participant in all three arms takes the same survey — a sample of items that were live in their field during the trial (bulletin-board entries, preprints, posts, tools), mixed with real items that never surfaced and a few fabricated decoys. Two questions per item: do you remember encountering this, and is it useful to your work?
The readout is a pair of lifts. Recall lift: how much more often the grounded arm remembers real items than the other two, with the decoys catching over-claiming. Usefulness lift: the share of remembered items rated useful, which separates "the Router showed me more" from "the Router showed me more that mattered." Because participation logs traces, the survey also runs retroactively — the items that would have surfaced for a researcher in an ungrounded arm are knowable, so every arm can be scored against the same item pool. What we expect to move: quality climbs the whole ladder, A to B to C; recall barely moves from A to B and jumps at C. That jump is the delta the Router owns. Try the survey prototype to see how answers become the number.
The trial needs only public data: a corpus built before a single researcher types a word. The headline test is Arm C against Arm B. The window is one month — three if the power analysis demands it — and grounding beats the ungrounded frontier or the project stops, having spent a fraction of its $110,000 tranche-gated budget.
One bias, named in advance: the cohort shares a community and talks, so whatever the Router teaches Arm C leaks into Arm B over coffee. Contamination drags the measured difference toward zero, which makes any surviving margin a floor: the true effect can only be larger. Participation requires study telemetry only, with a per-session off-the-record toggle, and how often the toggle gets used is itself reported. Contributing to the commons stays voluntary per entry, and the seat is funded either way. Seats are the small part of the $110,000; most of it is rater time, engineering, and the tranche reserve. Budget, milestones, and funding detail: the pilot memo.
Next steps
  1. Help co-design the study. The arms, endpoints, and rater rubric above are a draft until the anchor group marks them up; the pre-registration gets written together, with IRB approval held at a partner university so the study runs under institutional cover.
  2. Recruit for it. Fifty seats — 25 established researchers, 25 grad students — drawn from one community. The bottleneck is introductions, not budget.
  3. Deploy it. The corpus builds from public data before anyone types a word, seats provision over MCP into the tools the cohort already uses, and the trial window of real sessions decides the delta.
  4. Publish the result. A pre-registered grounding trial is publishable whichever way the delta falls — a natural IC3 (Initiative for Cryptocurrencies and Contracts) contribution, if the anchor group wants it there.
What a win would mean

What a positive result means

For the tools

Grounding becomes a measured lever

A thin context-and-tools layer, built by a small team with no training run, measurably improves the best research environment a frontier lab ships. If it holds, the same recipe should transfer to other fields, since it grounds generic agents in whatever people and literature a field already has. We test it first in cryptography on purpose, one of the heaviest-adopting and most AI-savvy fields, so clearing that bar is the hard case; whether the gain carries to lighter-adoption fields is for replication to show.

For the incentives

Sharing becomes rational

Attested timestamps protect priority without publication, which removes the main reason researchers keep unfinished ideas to themselves. They can talk months earlier. The group chat the incentive structure has been suppressing becomes safe to have: credit goes to whoever is first through the territory, and the timestamp is the flag.

For the record

Reasoning stops evaporating

The dead ends, the killed constructions, and the counterexamples that drove the work get preserved in the researcher's own words, owned by the researcher, verifiable by anyone. The commons compounds: every approved entry and every confirmed thread sharpens the map for the next researcher.

For the field

A counterweight to centralization

Follow the trend to its end: the field's best researcher gets hired by a frontier lab, does all the research, and nobody else needs funding — a tragic outcome for anyone who thinks science is a commons, and the direction the numbers above already point: when AI professors left for industry, the leavers' share of the field's citations rose from about 5% to nearly 19%. Jeff Hammerbacher watched the best minds of his generation measure how to get people to click on ads; the same absorption is coming for science, at better salaries. Some thinking has to be insulated from the pressure of selling things, and that insulation now requires infrastructure: a connective layer that is neutral and researcher-owned, funded at $300-a-month institutional seats — the $200 frontier seat plus the Router layer — without a single researcher's data ever being the product.

The endgame
This trial is month one of a longer argument. The endgame: unlimited frontier tokens for PhD researchers and grad students to work on the world's hardest problems — together, as a network, outside the confines of the frontier labs — funded as a public good.
A blind-scored RCT with a clean Arm-C-minus-Arm-B delta is precisely the evidence that case needs. It prices what a grounded frontier seat is worth to science, researcher by researcher, and a priced result lets a public-goods funder — the Vitaliks of the world — underwrite seats at scale instead of on faith. The old promise of academia was that curiosity and hard work earned you the resources to pursue the problems you cared about; this is how that promise comes back. The trial buys the argument.
